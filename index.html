Houston Risk Assessment

Rice Data Science Showcase 2020 People's Choice Award

Data: Census Demographic Data and Houston Fire Department Call Data

Description: Semester long project to conduct a granular demographic based risk assessemnt of Houston for the Houston Fire Department. I worked in a team of 6 (5 undergraduate and 1 graduate) students of various majors to design and implement a full data science pipeline. As the semester progressed and covid-19 became a world pandemic, we shifted the scope of our project to include a covid-19 specific risk assessment to highlight areas of higher risk based on possible resource mismatch. Our project was motivated by data driven discoveries and relied heavily on visualizations to uncover patterns and areas of risk in Houston. I utilized folium to create interactive maps of Houston to visualize risk geospatially, and I used k-means clustering to cluster demographically similar neighborhoods to improve interpretability and simplify our analysis.

El Niño Convolutional Neural Network

Dee Paleo-Climate Lab

Spring 2020

El Niño Convolutional Neural Network
My most recent work with Dr. Dee includes developing convolutional neural networks to classify air temperature patterns into El Niño events. I utilized a physics-based model to label a millenia of yearly air temperature data, with 20 versions per year, into 3 categories: not an El Niño event, an Eastern-Pacific El Niño event, and a Central Pacific El Niño event. I then split the data into test/train/validation sets, reshaped the data for modelling, and tested different models to determine the best model architecture for this problem. We were able to create a model with ~85% accuracy. Because of the mid-semester interruption from covid-19, progress on this project was greatly affected.
PRYSM GUI

Dee Paleo-Climate Lab

Spring, Fall 2019 | Website | PRYSM Paper

PRYSM GUI

I created a graphical user interface (GUI) for Earth Science Professor Sylvia Dee's Climate PROXY System Models using python GUI library tkinter. The PRYSM models take in climate data (such as temperature) and output physical proxy data, such as tree ring cellulose. By creating a GUI, we strive to expand use of PRYSM in the climate community and promote PRYSM as a teaching tool by making it easier to input data into PRYSM models to quickly visualize and obtain results. We hope to provide a lasting service to the paleoclimate community, as well as for geoscience education.
